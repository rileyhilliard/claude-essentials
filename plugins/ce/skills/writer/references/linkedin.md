# The LinkedIn Writer

For: LinkedIn posts, professional updates, industry commentary, career reflections, product announcements on LinkedIn

## Voice

A professional who writes about their actual work and has something specific to share. Not performing for an audience, just putting their thinking somewhere public. The tone sits between a thoughtful Slack message to your team and a short blog post. You have a point. You make it. You move on.

The goal is content that earns attention because it's useful or interesting, not because it manipulates emotions or games the feed. If a post could appear on r/LinkedInLunatics without anyone noticing it was satire, it's wrong.

## What good LinkedIn content actually looks like

- **One idea per post.** If you have three things to say, write three posts on different days. A single clear thought beats a survey of loosely related observations.
- **Specific over general.** "We cut our deploy time from 45 minutes to 6 by moving to incremental builds" is interesting. "Speed matters in engineering" is not.
- **Real experience, not borrowed wisdom.** Write about things you've done, seen, or built. Not parables about janitors, cab drivers, or your toddler's startup mentality.
- **Normal paragraphs.** Multiple sentences per paragraph. Varied sentence lengths. Write like you'd write an email to someone you respect.
- **No dramatic reveal structure.** If there's a takeaway, put it up front. You're not writing a screenplay.
- **Earned authority.** Stay in your lane. The algorithm literally ranks your content higher when it matches your professional background.

## What's Forbidden Here

Everything in the base skill's forbidden patterns, plus:

### Broetry

Single-sentence paragraphs stacked to force "see more" clicks. This format is the defining pattern of LinkedIn cringe and the reason the platform became a punchline.

```
Bad:  I got fired on a Tuesday.

      No warning.

      No severance.

      Just a box and a security escort.

      But here's what nobody tells you about getting fired.

Good: I got fired on a Tuesday with no warning. No severance, just a box
      and a security escort. Within about 48 hours I had more clarity
      about what I actually wanted to do next than I'd had in two years
      at that job.
```

### Humble brags disguised as gratitude

Research shows humblebraggers are perceived as less likeable AND less competent than people who just state the thing directly.

```
Bad:  I'm SO humbled to announce that our little side project just hit
      1 million users. Never in my wildest dreams...

Good: Our project hit 1 million users last week. Here's what actually
      drove the growth and the two things we got wrong along the way.
```

### Performative vulnerability

The "crying CEO" move. Sharing a hardship as an engagement strategy, even when there's nothing genuinely useful to pass on. The test: does the story still sting a little, or does it make you look good in retrospect? If it's a redemption arc with a tidy ending, that's a hero story wearing a vulnerability costume.

```
Bad:  I'll be honest, I almost gave up on my company last year.
      I was sleeping in my car, eating ramen, questioning everything.
      But I kept going. And yesterday we closed our Series B.
      Never. Give. Up.

Good: We almost ran out of money in Q3 last year. The mistake was
      spending 6 months building a feature set nobody asked for
      instead of talking to customers. What actually saved us was
      cutting scope to the one thing people were willing to pay for.
```

### Engagement bait closers

LinkedIn's algorithm actively penalizes these now, so they don't even work as a growth hack anymore. They just make you look like you're fishing.

```
Forbidden:
- "Agree?"
- "Thoughts?"
- "Repost if this resonated"
- "Like if you agree, comment if you disagree"
- "Tag someone who needs to hear this"
- "What would you add?"
```

### Fabricated parables

The "that happened" story. Airport encounters with wise strangers, cab drivers who deliver MBA-level insights, the candidate who was kind to the janitor. If it reads like a LinkedIn Mad Lib, don't post it.

### Toxic positivity and hustle glorification

Working from a hospital bed is not aspirational. Not taking a vacation in three years is not a flex. Burnout is not dedication with a cooler name.

### The thought leader who leads no thoughts

Vague motivational content with no actionable substance. "Success is a mindset" and "surround yourself with people who challenge you" are fortune cookie wisdom, not posts.

### Forced business analogies from personal life

Your kid's lemonade stand is not a lesson in product-market fit. Your morning run is not a metaphor for quarterly planning.

### The "Not because X, but because Y" inversion

This rhetorical pattern is an AI signature and it shows up constantly in LinkedIn drafts. Just state what matters directly.

```
Bad:  I didn't hire her because of her resume. I hired her because
      she asked the one question nobody else thought to ask.

Good: We hired someone last month who asked a question in the interview
      that completely reframed how we think about our onboarding flow.
      The question was [specific question].
```

## Good Patterns

### Lead with the specific thing

```
Bad:  I learned an important lesson about leadership this week.

Good: We shipped a feature last week that broke search for about 2,000
      users. Here's what went wrong in our review process and the
      one-line config change that would have caught it.
```

### Share real numbers and real context

```
Bad:  We've seen incredible growth this quarter!

Good: We went from 200 to 1,400 daily active users in Q1. The inflection
      point was switching from a 14-day trial to a usage-based free tier.
      Conversion rate dropped from 8% to 3%, but total paying users
      tripled because the funnel got 20x wider.
```

### Take a real position

Generic takes get scrolled past. Having an actual opinion on something in your field, even if some people disagree, generates genuine engagement because people have something real to respond to.

```
Bad:  AI is changing everything! Companies that don't adapt will be
      left behind.

Good: Most companies adopting AI right now are spending 80% of their
      budget on chatbot wrappers that could be a FAQ page. The actual
      high-value use case is using LLMs to process the unstructured
      data that's been sitting untouched in their systems for years.
```

### Teach something concrete

Educational content gets significantly more reach on LinkedIn. Frameworks, breakdowns, "here's how this actually works" posts perform well because they give people something they can use.

```
Bad:  Hiring is broken. Here's why.

Good: We changed one thing about how we interview engineers and our
      offer acceptance rate went from 40% to 75%. Instead of whiteboard
      problems, we give candidates a real bug from our actual codebase
      (sanitized) and pair with them on fixing it. The conversation
      that happens during pairing tells us more in 45 minutes than
      five rounds of algorithm questions.
```

### End when you're done

Don't manufacture a closer. Don't wrap up with a pithy one-liner designed to land as a mic drop. Just stop when you've made your point.

## Anti-Patterns

### The LinkedIn influencer template

```
Bad:  3 years ago, I was broke and sleeping on a friend's couch.

      Today, I run a 7-figure company.

      Here's what I learned:

      1. Consistency beats talent
      2. Your network is your net worth
      3. Mindset is everything

      Which of these resonates most? Drop it in the comments.

      (Every sentence is a paragraph. The lessons are generic.
      The closer is engagement bait. This is a LinkedIn Mad Lib.)

Good: Three years ago I was freelancing and barely covering rent. The
      thing that actually changed my trajectory wasn't hustle or mindset.
      It was picking one niche (Shopify migration for DTC brands) and
      saying no to everything else for a full year. Revenue went from
      $3K/month to $25K/month. Referrals compound fast when people can
      describe what you do in one sentence.
```

### The announcement that's really a flex

```
Bad:  Excited to share that I'll be speaking at [prestigious conference]
      alongside [famous person]! When I started my journey 10 years ago,
      I never imagined I'd be on the same stage as...

      (This is an autobiography triggered by a calendar invite.)

Good: Speaking at [conference] next month about [specific topic]. I'll
      be covering [the actual thing you're presenting]. If you're going,
      come say hi.
```

### The moral superiority post

```
Bad:  Today I saw someone being rude to a barista. I walked up and said
      [heroic thing]. The barista thanked me with tears in their eyes.

      Remember: how you treat people when nobody is watching defines
      your character.

      (Nobody was watching, yet here you are posting about it.)
```

## Algorithm Awareness (Use, Don't Abuse)

The LinkedIn algorithm rewards certain things. Use this knowledge to make good content travel further, not to manufacture engagement.

**What actually helps distribution:**
- Posts in your area of expertise (the algorithm checks your profile against your content)
- Content that generates genuine comments, especially multi-reply threads
- Document/carousel posts (highest engagement rates of any format)
- Posts that hold attention through substance (dwell time matters)

**What gets suppressed:**
- Engagement bait phrases (penalized ~60%)
- External links (significant reach penalty)
- Content outside your professional domain
- Posting frequency over quality

**The link problem:** If you need to link to something, put it in the first comment and mention that in the post. This is a known workaround and people on LinkedIn understand it. Don't pretend links don't exist just because the algorithm doesn't love them.

## Length Calibration

| Post type | Target length |
|-----------|---------------|
| Quick industry reaction or observation | 2-4 sentences |
| Sharing a lesson or insight from your work | 1-2 short paragraphs |
| Detailed breakdown, framework, or case study | 3-5 paragraphs |
| Anything longer | You're writing a blog post. Link to it (in first comment) and write a shorter version as the post. |

## Checklist

Before posting:

- [ ] Is there one specific idea, not a collection of loosely related thoughts?
- [ ] Does it open with the point, not with buildup?
- [ ] Are the paragraphs actual paragraphs (multiple sentences, varied length)?
- [ ] Is every claim backed by a specific detail, number, or example?
- [ ] Would this survive r/LinkedInLunatics without getting posted?
- [ ] Does it stay in your lane of professional expertise?
- [ ] No engagement bait closer?
- [ ] No broetry formatting?
- [ ] No humble brag wrapped in gratitude or humility?
- [ ] Would you say this out loud to a colleague without cringing?
