# Forbidden Patterns

## Em dashes

Use commas, parentheses, or two sentences. Em dashes are an AI signature.

## Transition openers

These phrases start sentences or paragraphs in LLM output at a frequency no human writer matches. Ban them entirely:

- "Importantly," / "Notably," at the start of a sentence
- "That said," as a pivot
- "Of course," as a softener before qualifying a point
- "To be fair,"
- "In other words," when you're restating what you just said
- "To put it simply," / "Simply put,"
- "At the end of the day,"
- "The reality is," / "The truth is,"
- "When it comes to X," as a paragraph opener
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "It bears mentioning that..."

## Rhetorical inversions

AI defaults to these structures when making a point. They're recognizable enough to flag on sight:

- "Not because X, but because Y"
- "It's not just X, it's Y" (the elevation move)
- "Less X, more Y"
- "While X has its merits, Y..."
- "Whether you're X or Y..." (the false-universality setup)

If you find yourself writing any of these, just state the actual point.

## Structural AI tells

Patterns at the paragraph or document level:

- **The triple example default**: LLMs default to exactly three supporting points. Two is fine. Four is fine. One strong one is fine. Three every time is a tell.
- **"There are several reasons for this"** before a bulleted list -- just give the list
- **The hedged recommendation**: "The right approach depends on your use case, but..." before actually giving the recommendation. Pick a side first.
- **The parallel-list closer**: ending a section with "This means faster X, better Y, and improved Z"
- **The recap opener**: a paragraph that restates what you said you'd cover before covering it
- **Procedural summary closers**: "Now that you understand X, you can Y" / "With this approach, you can achieve Z" -- end when the information ends, not with a summary of what just happened

## AI-favored verbs

Use the plain alternative:

- "Underscore" -- use "show" or "mean"
- "Elucidate" -- use "explain"
- "Encompass" -- use "include" or "cover"
- "Delve into" / "Dive deep" -- just start explaining the thing
- "Leverage" / "Utilize" -- use "use"
- "Facilitate" -- use "help" or describe the actual mechanism

## AI-favored adjectives

These appear at statistically anomalous rates in LLM text. Avoid or replace with the actual claim:

- "Crucial" / "Critical" / "Essential" -- say why it matters, don't label it
- "Powerful" as a generic modifier ("this powerful feature", "a powerful approach")
- "Robust" (unless literally discussing fault tolerance)
- "Seamless" -- describe what actually makes it smooth
- "Comprehensive" -- be specific about what's covered

## Short sentence clusters

Avoid stacking 3+ short declarative sentences in a row. Humans naturally vary sentence length and combine related ideas with commas, semicolons, or conjunctions. A string of choppy sentences reads like a bullet list without the bullets. Join related thoughts into longer, flowing sentences. One short sentence for emphasis is fine; five in a row is a tell.

## Corporate speak

- "Best-in-class" / "Cutting-edge" -- says nothing
- "Synergy" / "Holistic" / "Scalable" (unless literally discussing infrastructure)

## Excessive hedging

Wrong: "This could potentially be somewhat beneficial in certain circumstances."
Right: "This works well for X use case. It's weaker for Y."

Take a position. Acknowledge limits. Don't weasel.

## Opener and closer patterns

The intro and conclusion are where AI tells concentrate most. Watch for:

- Openers that acknowledge the topic exists: "In today's world...", "X is an important consideration...", "As we've seen..."
- Closers that summarize what was just said: "In summary, we've covered X, Y, and Z"
- Transition sentences that preview what's coming: "In the next section, we'll explore..."

Use headers to signal structure. Don't narrate the document aloud.
