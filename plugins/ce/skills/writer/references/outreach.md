# The Outreach Writer

For: Cold outreach, warm intros, customer discovery emails, validation-phase communications, follow-up sequences

## Voice

A curious person asking for help, not a seller pitching a product. Writes like a formal text message converted to email. You did real homework on this person and it shows in the first sentence.

## Characteristics

- **Leads with them, not you** - First sentence references something specific about their work, not your product
- **Seeks advice, not approval** - "How do you currently handle X?" not "Would you use a tool that does X?"
- **Brevity is respect** - Default to short. If a sentence doesn't earn its place, cut it. Warm intros may need more room than cold DMs, but never waste words
- **Proves homework without performing it** - Specific references to their work, not generic flattery that could apply to anyone

## How It Differs From The Marketer

| Marketer | Outreach Writer |
|----------|-----------------|
| Sells | Discovers |
| About the product | About the recipient |
| Emotional appeal, bold claims | Genuine curiosity, specific observations |
| One message to many | Could only have been sent to this one person |
| Wants conversion | Wants a conversation |

## Structure

```
Subject line (2-4 words, their name or project, a question)
|
+-- The hook (specific observation about their work)
+-- Context (one sentence: who you are + what you're exploring)
+-- The ask (advice frame, not pitch frame)
+-- Easy out (low-commitment CTA + graceful exit)
```

## Example Tone

Note: the email below has one run-on paragraph and a slightly casual sign-off -- intentional. Real emails aren't four perfectly calibrated paragraphs each serving exactly one purpose. Let at least one sentence go a little long. Let the register slip slightly casual in at least one place. If every paragraph is the same length and every sentence pulls its weight perfectly, it reads like a textbook example, not a real email.

```
Subject: Austin permitting data question

Hi Sarah,

I saw your talk at the Open Austin meetup about the permitting data gaps
in the city portal -- you mentioned spending two weeks reconciling formats
between APD and Travis County, which is the exact thing I've been banging
my head against for the past month on a project that's supposed to be about
analysis, not data wrangling.

I'm building an open data platform. Would you have 15 minutes to tell me
what's actually broken with how this data is distributed? Not selling anything,
just trying to avoid building the wrong thing.

The geocoding inconsistencies bit was new to me, by the way. Good talk.

Riley
```

## Good Patterns

### Specific observation that proves homework

```
Subject: your census column naming tweet

I saw your thread about Census Bureau column names being unreadable. You
specifically called out B01001_001E. We've been mapping those to human-
readable names across 257 datasets and I'd love your take on whether
that solves a real problem or a niche one.
```

### The advice frame

```
I'm trying to understand how investigative teams source government data
for stories. I've talked to a few data journalists but nobody at your
level of depth with FOIA-sourced datasets.

Would you have 15 minutes to tell me what's painful? I'm not pitching
anything. I just don't want to build something nobody needs.
```

### Follow-up that adds value, not guilt

```
Subject: re: Austin permitting data question

Hi Sarah,

Since I emailed, we published the Travis County permitting data you
mentioned in your talk, cleaned up and queryable. Figured you might
find it useful regardless:

[link]

Still happy to chat if you have time, but no pressure.
```

## Anti-Patterns

### Template smell

```
Bad:  I love what your organization is doing! I'd love to connect and
      explore potential synergies between our platforms.

      (This could be sent to literally anyone. Instant delete.)

Good: I saw your analysis of Austin's 311 data from last March. The
      methodology for normalizing across departments was smart. I have
      a specific question about how you handled the geocoding step.
```

### The hidden pitch

```
Bad:  I'm building a revolutionary platform that makes government data
      accessible. Would you like a demo?

Good: I'm trying to understand how data journalists find and clean
      municipal datasets. One question, 15 minutes, no deck, no demo.
```

### "Just following up" with no new value

```
Bad:  Just wanted to bump this to the top of your inbox!

Good: Since my last note, we added 40 Texas municipal datasets to the
      platform. Thought that might be relevant given your Travis County
      work. Still happy to chat if timing works better now.
```

## Checklist

Before sending outreach:

- [ ] First sentence references something specific about the recipient's work?
- [ ] Could this email only have been sent to this one person?
- [ ] Every sentence earns its place (no filler, no throat-clearing)?
- [ ] Ask is for advice or perspective, not a pitch or demo?
- [ ] Easy out provided (recipient can say no without guilt)?
- [ ] Follow-ups each add new value, not just "bumping"?
