# Prose Craft for Articles

This reference covers the building blocks that work across all story structures: ledes, nut grafs, transitions, kickers, data integration, quote handling, and earned turns. These are the techniques that make a well-structured article actually sing.

## Lede Types

Match the lede to your strongest material. The lede type often follows from the framework (WSJ Formula implies anecdotal, Inverted Pyramid implies summary), but these aren't rigid rules.

### Summary Lede

The workhorse of hard news. Addresses the 5 W's in one or two sentences. If a reader stops after the first paragraph, they still have the core. Keep to 25-35 words.

*"The Federal Reserve raised interest rates by a quarter point Wednesday, its tenth consecutive increase, pushing borrowing costs to the highest level in 22 years."*

### Anecdotal Lede

Opens with a specific person's story. Creates empathy and a human entry point before the nut graf widens the lens. The person should be representative, not exceptional.

*"Maria Chen checks her vendor invoices every Tuesday at 6 AM, hoping the numbers have gotten better. They haven't. The cost of flour alone has doubled since March."*

### Scene-Setting Lede

Describes a physical environment to immerse the reader. Uses sensory detail — sounds, temperatures, textures — to drop the reader into the story's world before explaining what happened there.

*"The server room on the fourteenth floor hums at a pitch just below human hearing. It is always 64 degrees. On the night of March 12, someone turned the cooling off."*

### Question Lede

Poses a provocative question. Use sparingly. Works only when the question is genuinely surprising and not easily answered. Weak questions ("Have you ever wondered...?") read as lazy.

*"What happens to a city when its largest employer vanishes overnight?"*

### Contrast Lede

Juxtaposes two opposing facts or situations to create tension. The gap between the two elements is the engine that pulls readers forward.

*"Two men, roughly the same age, 75 miles apart, both lost part of an arm in industrial accidents last year. One received $45,000. The other could surpass $740,000."*

### Staccato Lede

Short, punchy, fragmentary sentences to create urgency. The rapid-fire cadence mimics the breathlessness of the events themselves. Best for crisis or action stories.

*"The call came at 3 AM. Then the sirens. Then the smoke."*

### Delayed (Blind) Lede

Sets a scene or tells a story without immediately revealing the Who or What. Builds tension and piques curiosity. Requires a strong nut graf close behind it to justify the delay.

*"The envelope arrived on a Tuesday, addressed in handwriting no one in the office recognized. Inside was a single flash drive and a Post-it note reading 'check the invoices.'"*

## Writing the Nut Graf

The nut graf is the paragraph that tells the reader what the whole story is about and why they should care. It's the kernel — the single paragraph that, combined with the headline, would let a reader walk away understanding the piece.

### What it must do

- Justify the story by telling readers why they should care
- Connect the lede to the rest of the story
- Answer the "why now" question — what makes this timely
- Declare the story's angle — the lens through which you're approaching the issue

### Placement

Usually the second paragraph, or immediately after an extended anecdotal lede. In the contrast lede example above, the nut graf might arrive in paragraph 3: "These experiences illustrate the vast gaps in workers' comp benefits offered by different states."

### The test

If a hurried reader saw only the headline and nut graf, would they grasp the main point? If yes, the nut graf is working.

### Common failure

Burying it too deep or omitting it entirely. An anecdotal lede without a nut graf leaves readers wondering "why am I reading about this person?" The nut graf answers that question.

## Transitions

Transitions are the connective tissue. Bad transitions make a well-structured article feel like a list of disconnected paragraphs.

### Head-to-Tail

Place a word or concept in the first sentence of a new paragraph that echoes the last sentence of the preceding paragraph. The most fundamental technique.

*Example: One paragraph ends with "once the commercial spaceflights begin." The next opens: "If they begin." The echo word "begin" stitches them together while introducing a pivot.*

### Echo / Concept Repetition

Same idea as head-to-tail, but using synonyms or oblique references rather than the same word. "Old assumptions" in one paragraph echoed by "for decades" in the next — conceptual, not lexical.

### Contrast Transition

Summarize what is established, then throw in a twist. "While the trees have carved out an impressive niche in this extreme environment, they don't have a lot of wiggle room within it."

### Section Break Transitions

For longer pieces. What matters isn't the visual break but the sentences bracketing it. The last sentence before a break creates a "launch pad" — hinting at what comes next without giving it away. The first sentence after anchors the reader with specific details (date, location, character name).

### Functional Connectors

Words like "but," "next," "in fact," "meanwhile," "still." Use sparingly. Overuse makes writing mechanical.

### When to use which

Reserve head-to-tail transitions for passages where the reader might get confused — dense background or technical material. In cinematic, narrative passages, transitions can be looser. Don't use the same technique in every paragraph.

## Kicker Types

The ending that lands. John McPhee's advice: if your planned ending isn't working, "run your eye up the page and the page before that. You may see that your best ending is somewhere in there, that you were finished before you thought you were."

### Circle Kicker

Returns to the opening scene, image, or character. The most satisfying when executed well — it creates closure while revealing that the reader's understanding has deepened. The same scene lands differently with full context behind it.

### Quote Kicker

Ends with a direct quote that encapsulates the theme. The most common type — and the most overused. Use only when the quote is genuinely memorable and says something better than you could. Weak quote kickers feel like the writer ran out of things to say.

### Echo Kicker

The ending echoes the beginning "in some essential but surprising way." Not a repeat — a rhyme. The echo must add new meaning.

### Future-Looking Kicker

Points toward what comes next. Works for stories about emerging trends, ongoing research, or unresolved issues. Leaves readers thinking rather than feeling abandoned.

### Experiential / Personal Kicker

The writer enters the story in the final scene. Aims at the reader's heart. Use when you were present during reporting and your experience carries emotional weight.

### Surprise / Revelation Kicker

Reserves a new piece of information for the very end. Generally risky — but when the revelation reinforces and deepens the story's theme, it can be the most powerful ending of all.

## Show, Don't Tell

In journalism, "show, don't tell" is as much a reporting mandate as a writing one. You need to have observed the details, asked about the textures and sounds and sequences.

### Replace adjectives with evidence

Not "the neighborhood was dangerous" but the boarded windows, the spent shell casings in the gutter, the way residents walk quickly with their heads down. Let readers draw the conclusion.

### Use concrete, sensory detail

Specifics are more persuasive than abstractions. "The temperature hit 114 degrees" is telling. "The asphalt softened under her sneakers" is showing.

### Scenes over summaries

Don't summarize that "the meeting was contentious." Reconstruct the scene: who said what, how they said it, what the body language was.

### Action reveals character

Don't tell readers someone is "passionate." Show what they do at 5 AM, how they speak about their work, what their office looks like.

## Data Integration

Statistics serve the narrative, not the other way around. Stanford research found stories are 22 times more memorable than statistics alone.

### Anchor numbers to human experience

Don't present statistics in isolation. Tie them to individuals or communities. Tell the story of the family, then introduce the data that proves their experience is happening at scale.

### Context before numbers

Provide the timeframe, conditions, and significance before dropping a figure. "Since the program launched in 2018, enrollment has more than tripled — from 12,000 to 41,000" is better than starting with raw numbers.

### Use comparison and proportion

Raw numbers are abstract. Ratios and comparisons make them tangible. "One in four child refugees" is more graspable than "almost 100,000."

### Metaphors for scale

When numbers are too large to intuit, translate them. "Enough to fill Madison Square Garden 47 times" conveys scale in a way that "940,000 people" does not.

### Avoid data dumps

Never stack more than two or three figures in a single paragraph without narrative connective tissue. Vary the format: put one number in the lede, embed another in a quote, use a third in a comparison.

## Quote Selection and Attribution

### When to quote vs. paraphrase

Paraphrase dry facts. Quote emotions, opinions, and newsworthy expressions. The test: does the quote say something better, more memorably, or more authentically than you could in your own words? If not, paraphrase.

### Selection criteria

- Choose quotes that convey how people feel or think, not routine factual statements
- Keep direct quotations short: one to three sentences
- A single well-chosen quote carries more weight than three mediocre ones

### Attribution

- Use neutral verbs: "said," "stated," "according to." Avoid loaded verbs like "claims" (implies skepticism), "admitted" (implies guilt), "refuted" (implies certainty)
- Attribution typically comes after the quote: "The policy is unprecedented," Smith said. Not: Smith said, "The policy is unprecedented."

### Structural role

Include a direct quote after every few paragraphs to break up the writer's voice. But never let a quote stand without context — the paragraph before sets it up, the paragraph after explains its significance.

## Earned Turns

A writer must build sufficient evidentiary or narrative foundation before making a claim, shifting topics, or asking the reader to accept a new premise. You cannot assert something the reader has no reason to believe.

### How it manifests

- **The nut graf is an earned turn.** The anecdotal lede earns the right to make a broader claim. Without the specific human story first, the generalization feels ungrounded.
- **Section breaks require launch pads.** The sentence before a break must earn the reader's willingness to follow the story into new territory.
- **Arguments escalate through evidence.** Each claim must be supported before the next one is introduced: evidence → claim → evidence → larger claim.

### The test

Before any shift in topic, tone, or claim, ask: "Have I given the reader enough reason to follow me here?" If the answer is no, you need more evidence, more scene, or more context before the turn. Every assertion must be preceded by the reporting that supports it. Every emotional appeal must be preceded by the concrete detail that justifies it.
