# The Braided Narrative

Discussed at Nieman Storyboard and used by publications like The Atavist and The New Yorker. Also called "broken narrative" or "woven narrative." The structure interweaves two or more storylines that almost stand alone but gain meaning from juxtaposition.

Best for: complex investigative stories, pieces with parallel characters or timelines, stories where meaning emerges from seeing two narratives side by side.

---

## Structure

### 1. Identify Your Threads

Find two (or occasionally three) storylines that can almost stand alone but resonate when placed together. The threads might be:

- Two people on opposite sides of the same issue
- The same problem across two time periods
- A policy and its human consequence, told in parallel
- Two investigations converging on the same discovery

The threads don't need to connect literally — thematic resonance is enough.

### 2. Open with One Thread — 2-4 paragraphs

Establish the first storyline. Give the reader enough to be invested in this character or situation before you cut away.

### 3. Alternate Between Threads

Weave back and forth. Each time you switch threads, the reader should feel both the pull of the story they're leaving and the pull of the one they're entering.

Transitions between threads are critical. Use the last line of one section to echo or contrast with the first line of the next. The cut should feel purposeful, not arbitrary.

### 4. Build Toward Intersection

The threads should build toward a point where they intersect, collide, or illuminate each other. This convergence doesn't have to be literal (the characters don't have to meet), but the reader should understand why these two stories were told together.

### 5. Resolve

The threads can merge into a single closing, or each can get its own ending. What matters is that the reader feels the combined meaning — the whole is greater than the sum of the parts.

---

## Why It Works

The braided structure creates meaning through juxtaposition. When you show a policy debate in Washington alongside a family living under that policy, neither thread says what both threads say together. The reader makes the connection themselves, and connections the reader makes feel more powerful than connections the writer states.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Threads that don't resonate.** If the reader can't feel why these two stories belong in the same article, the structure falls apart. The connection can be subtle, but it must exist.
- **Unequal investment.** If one thread is dramatically more interesting than the other, the reader will resent every cut away from it. Both threads need to pull their weight.
- **Too many threads.** Two is standard. Three is possible with discipline. Four is almost always a mess. Each additional thread reduces the time you can spend in any one, weakening all of them.
- **Arbitrary switching.** Don't alternate mechanically (Thread A, Thread B, Thread A, Thread B). Switch when the story demands it — when a scene in one thread has reached a moment of tension, revelation, or pause that creates energy for cutting to the other.
