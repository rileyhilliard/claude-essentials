# The Modern Inverted Pyramid

The workhorse of news journalism, used heavily by *The New York Times*, AP, Reuters, and every wire service. The core rule: front-load value. Write in descending order of importance so a reader can stop at any moment and still have the main point.

Best for: breaking news, policy announcements, reports, analyses, any piece where the headline fact is the strongest material.

---

## Structure

### 1. The Hard Lede — 1-2 sentences

The most critical information in the fewest words. Answer the essential Who, What, Where, When. This is not a place for scene-setting or narrative. It's the headline expanded into a sentence.

Keep it to 25-35 words. Every word must be functional. If you can cut a word without losing meaning, cut it.

**Example:** "The Federal Reserve raised interest rates by a quarter point Wednesday, its tenth consecutive increase, pushing borrowing costs to the highest level in 22 years."

### 2. The Nut Graf — 1-2 paragraphs

Explain the Why and How. Ground the breaking news in context: what caused this, what are the immediate consequences, what are the stakes. This is where you transform a fact into a story.

The nut graf in an inverted pyramid is more analytical than in the WSJ Formula. You're not zooming out from an anecdote — you're deepening a fact.

### 3. Main Body — varies

Direct quotes from primary sources, crucial supporting statistics, and a narrative play-by-play of the event. Each paragraph should be able to stand alone. Each should be less essential than the one before it.

This is the discipline of the inverted pyramid: if an editor needed to cut the article from the bottom up for space, nothing vital would be lost. Every paragraph you write lower in the piece is a paragraph you're saying "this is less important than everything above it."

### 4. Secondary Context

History of how we got here, past precedents, related developments. Readers who've made it this far want depth. Give them the background that enriches their understanding but isn't essential to the headline.

### 5. The Taper

Minor details, administrative information, forward-looking quotes from secondary sources. If this section disappeared entirely, the article would still function.

---

## When to Modernize It

The pure inverted pyramid can feel mechanical. Modern practitioners (especially at the Times and Washington Post) often hybridize it:

- **Add a scene or anecdote after the hard lede** to give the reader something human to hold onto, then continue with the pyramid structure below.
- **Use the "nut graf duo"** — a hard lede paired with a nut graf that reframes the significance, creating a 1-2 punch at the top before the descent.
- **End with a strong quote or forward-looking paragraph** rather than trailing off into minor details.

These tweaks preserve the front-loading discipline while making the reading experience less like a government report.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Burying the lede.** If your most important fact appears in paragraph 3, you've failed the structure. Move it up.
- **Starting with context.** "In recent years, concerns about..." is a setup, not a lede. Start with the news.
- **Treating all paragraphs as equal.** The entire logic of this structure is a hierarchy of importance. If you can't rank your paragraphs by priority, you don't yet understand your own story.
- **Ending abruptly.** The taper can feel like the article just... stops. A closing quote or forward-looking sentence prevents that.
