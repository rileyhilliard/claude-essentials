# The WSJ Formula (Specific-to-General Feature Structure)

Also known as the "kebab" or "kabob" structure in some newsrooms.

Pioneered by *The Wall Street Journal* to make dense economic and business trends deeply engaging. The core move: take a massive, abstract issue and view it through the lens of a single person or scene.

Best for: trend pieces, policy stories, systemic issues, any feature where you can personalize the abstract.

---

## Structure

### 1. The Soft Lede (The Micro) — 1-3 paragraphs

Start with an anecdote, not data. Introduce a "poster child" — a specific person, business, or micro-event heavily impacted by the trend you want to discuss. Use sensory details to put the reader in their shoes.

The person should be representative, not exceptional. You're not profiling them — you're using their experience as a window into something bigger. The reader doesn't know that yet, and that's fine.

**What makes it work:** concrete detail. Not "a small business owner struggling with costs" but "Maria Chen checks her vendor invoices every Tuesday at 6 AM, hoping the numbers have gotten better. They haven't."

### 2. The Nut Graf (The Macro) — 1-2 paragraphs

Zoom out. Explicitly connect the opening anecdote to the bigger picture. This paragraph answers: "Why does this individual's story matter right now? What is the broader trend or problem?"

The nut graf is the hinge of the entire piece. It transforms a personal anecdote into a story about something systemic. Without it, the reader wonders why they're reading about Maria Chen. With it, Maria Chen becomes the face of a nationwide trend.

**Example transition:** "Chen is one of roughly 400,000 small restaurant owners nationwide facing the same squeeze..."

### 3. The Body (The Evidence) — varies

Organize topically, not chronologically. Deliver:

- Hard data that proves the claim in your nut graf
- Expert quotes that add authority or nuance
- Historical context that shows how we got here
- Additional examples (briefly — you already have your main character)

Think of this section as building the case a lawyer would make. Each paragraph should advance the argument, not just add information.

### 4. The "To Be Sure" Section — 1-2 paragraphs

Place necessary disclosures, counterarguments, and opposing viewpoints here. This is where you acknowledge complexity without letting it undercut your lede's momentum.

This section is an act of intellectual honesty. It strengthens the piece by showing you've considered the other side. But keeping it in the bottom half prevents it from killing the reader's engagement before you've earned their attention.

### 5. The Circle Kicker (The Resolution) — 1 paragraph

Return to the person or scene from your soft lede. Give an update on their situation or use a final quote from them. The reader started with Maria Chen; they end with her too.

The best circle kickers don't just revisit — they reveal. The reader now understands Maria's situation in a way they couldn't at the start. The same scene lands differently with the full context behind it.

## Example Outline

**Topic:** Rising childcare costs are forcing parents out of the workforce.

1. **Soft Lede:** Sarah, a software engineer in Denver, describes the moment she realized her childcare costs exceeded her mortgage. She quit her job last month.
2. **Nut Graf:** Sarah's situation is playing out across the country. Since 2020, childcare costs have risen 35% nationally, outpacing wage growth in 47 states. An estimated 1.2 million parents have reduced work hours or left the workforce entirely.
3. **Body:** Bureau of Labor Statistics data on cost increases. Interview with an economist on the labor market impact. Policy history — how childcare subsidies were expanded during COVID, then expired. Comparison with European models.
4. **To Be Sure:** Some employers are responding with subsidies. Childcare costs vary dramatically by region. The trend may be self-correcting as new facilities open.
5. **Circle Kicker:** Sarah is now freelancing from home, earning 40% less. She's applied to three childcare centers for next fall. The waitlists are eight months long.

## Common Mistakes

- **Choosing someone too unusual.** The poster child must be representative. If their situation is unique, readers won't buy the nut graf's generalization.
- **Lingering too long in the anecdote.** The soft lede is 1-3 paragraphs. It's an appetizer, not the entree. Get to the nut graf before the reader starts wondering where you're going.
- **Forgetting the circle kicker.** The WSJ Formula without a circle kicker is just a feature with a nice opening. The return is what makes the structure click.
- **Body organized chronologically.** Unless the timeline is the point, organize by theme. Chronological bodies tend to meander.
