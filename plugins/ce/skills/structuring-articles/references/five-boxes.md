# The Five Boxes

Learned by Pulitzer Prize winner Rick Bragg from his editor Pat Farnan at the St. Petersburg Times. Popularized by Chip Scanlan at the Poynter Institute. A practical organizing tool for writers who have material and need structure fast.

Best for: deadline writing when you need to organize material quickly. Works for news features, profiles, and longer stories. Particularly effective when you have a pile of notes and don't know where to start.

---

## Structure

Draw five boxes. Fill each with bullet points of information, quotes, and statistics.

### Box 1 — The Lead

The image, detail, or moment that draws people into the story. This is your best material — the thing you'd tell a friend first.

### Box 2 — The Nut Graf

What the story is about and why it matters. The "so what" paragraph that justifies the entire piece.

### Box 3 — The Secondary Lead

A new image or detail (resembling a fresh lead) that precedes the bulk of the narrative. Think of this as a second hook — it re-engages readers as they move into the more detailed middle section.

### Box 4 — Supporting Details

Material that is less compelling but rounds out the story: context, background, additional sourcing, secondary quotes, historical notes. This is necessary but not gripping — which is why it goes here, after you've hooked the reader twice.

### Box 5 — The Kicker

A strong quote or image that leaves the reader with a lasting emotional impression. The ending should resonate, not summarize.

---

## How to Use It

This isn't a framework you think about in the abstract — it's a physical organizing tool.

1. Look at your notes, quotes, and research.
2. Draw five boxes on paper or in a doc.
3. Sort each piece of material into a box based on where it belongs.
4. Write from Box 1 through Box 5.

The power is in the sorting. Once material is in boxes, the blank page isn't blank anymore. You have a sequence. The writing becomes execution rather than invention.

---

## Why It Works

The Five Boxes solves the most common problem in deadline writing: you have plenty of material but no idea where to start or what goes where. The boxes impose structure without being rigid. Unlike the WSJ Formula or Inverted Pyramid, there are no strict rules about what kind of material goes in each box — just a rough hierarchy from "strongest" to "supporting" to "closing."

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Box 1 isn't your strongest material.** If the lead doesn't grab, nothing else matters. Be honest about what your best moment is — it's not always the first thing that happened.
- **Box 3 is empty.** The secondary lead is what separates a structured story from one that sags in the middle. Find a second strong moment to re-engage the reader.
- **Box 5 is a summary.** The kicker should be a moment, a quote, an image — not a recap of what the reader just read.
