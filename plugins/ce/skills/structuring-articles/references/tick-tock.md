# The Tick-Tock (Reconstruction)

A staple of political journalism, crisis reporting, and event reconstruction. The tick-tock narrates events in strict chronological order, often with timestamps, to give readers the experience of being inside the room as history unfolds.

Best for: major political decisions, crises, disasters, investigations, battles, any event where readers want to understand "how it happened" step by step.

---

## Structure

### 1. Establish the Event — 1-2 paragraphs

Set the stakes immediately. What is the significant moment or decision being reconstructed? The reader needs to know why they're about to read a minute-by-minute account.

### 2. Set Timeline Boundaries

Define the start and end points. "The 72 hours that led to..." or "From the first report at 6:14 AM to the president's address that evening." Clear boundaries create unity of time, place, and action.

### 3. Reconstruct Chronologically

Narrate events in strict time order with temporal markers ("At 3:15 AM," "By Tuesday morning," "Within the hour"). Each scene should:

- Place the reader in a specific location
- Identify who was present
- Reveal what was said or decided
- Show what happened next as a consequence

Layer in behind-the-scenes detail: what happened in private meetings, phone calls, closed-door deliberations. The power of the tick-tock is access — readers see what they couldn't have seen.

### 4. Build Scenes

Use fiction techniques applied to factual events: dialogue, character details, setting descriptions, point of view. The tick-tock is the "ultimate show-don't-tell conceit." Don't summarize what happened in the meeting — put the reader in the meeting.

### 5. Close with Resolution or Aftermath — 1-2 paragraphs

Show the outcome and its immediate consequences. The best tick-tock endings create a moment of reckoning: the reader now understands how it all happened and can judge for themselves whether it had to happen that way.

---

## Requirements

The tick-tock demands extensive sourcing. You need:

- Multiple interviews to reconstruct scenes credibly
- Specific times, locations, and details
- Enough sourcing that the reader trusts your reconstruction

If you can't reconstruct scenes with confidence, don't use this structure. A tick-tock built on thin sourcing reads like fiction, and not in a good way.

---

## Cautions

Columbia Journalism Review has noted that official sources (especially governments and corporations) have learned to exploit the tick-tock format, feeding journalists self-serving details that make their actions look decisive and competent. Both *The Wall Street Journal* and *The Washington Post* have pulled back from tick-tocks that rely on unverifiable official anecdotes, particularly in political coverage. Be skeptical of any tick-tock that makes everyone in the room look good. If your reconstruction relies primarily on one side's account, disclose that.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Insufficient sourcing.** If you're guessing at what happened behind closed doors, you don't have a tick-tock — you have historical fiction.
- **Monotonous pacing.** Not every minute is equally important. Speed through the uneventful stretches and slow down for the pivotal moments.
- **Missing stakes.** Without clear stakes established at the top, a chronological retelling is just a timeline. The reader needs to know why this sequence matters before they'll commit to following it.
