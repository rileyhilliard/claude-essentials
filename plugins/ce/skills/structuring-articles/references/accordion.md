# The Accordion

A structure that oscillates between the micro (individual experience) and the macro (data, trends, systemic context). Each zoom-in/zoom-out cycle covers a different facet of the issue. Named for the expanding-contracting motion.

Best for: data-driven stories with a compelling central character, long-form features that need both personal and systemic perspectives, stories where the human story validates the data and the data validates the human story.

---

## Structure

### 1. Opening Anecdote — 2-3 paragraphs

A strong scene from a central character. Establish who they are, what they're facing, and why the reader should care.

### 2. Nut Graf — 1-2 paragraphs

Broaden to the big picture. Connect the individual to the trend.

### 3. Alternating Zoom-In / Zoom-Out Cycles — varies

For each major aspect of the story:

**Zoom in:** A specific anecdote from the central character (or another affected individual) illustrating this particular facet. Use scene, dialogue, sensory detail.

**Zoom out:** Contextualize with data, expert quotes, broader trends. This is where you deliver the research that proves this facet matters beyond the individual.

Repeat for each major facet. Typically 3-4 cycles — enough to be thorough without becoming repetitive.

### 4. Closing Anecdote — 1-2 paragraphs

Return to the central character. Show where they are now, or use a final quote that encapsulates the story's theme.

---

## How It Differs from the WSJ Formula

The WSJ Formula has one anecdotal opening, one body of evidence, and one circle kicker. The Accordion uses the same person throughout, returning to them multiple times. Each return illuminates a different aspect of their experience.

Think of the WSJ Formula as: here's a person → here's the data → here's the person again. The Accordion is: here's a person → data → person again showing a new facet → more data → person again showing another facet → and so on.

---

## Why It Works

The alternation keeps both kinds of material fresh. Data without story becomes a government report. Story without data becomes a profile. By cycling between them, each validates the other. The data proves the anecdote isn't an outlier; the anecdote proves the data isn't abstract.

Stanford research found that stories are 22 times more memorable than statistics alone. The Accordion lets you have both.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Always zooming out to the same data.** Each zoom-out should cover a genuinely different facet. If every macro section says "and the numbers confirm this," you're repeating yourself.
- **Central character too passive.** The character needs to be doing things, experiencing things, making decisions. A passive character makes the zoom-in sections feel like filler between the real content.
- **Too many cycles.** Three or four zoom-in/zoom-out cycles is the sweet spot. More than that and the structure starts to feel mechanical.
