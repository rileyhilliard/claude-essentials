# The Diamond

A classic feature-writing structure that eases readers into complex or unfamiliar territory. The shape tells the story: start narrow (one person, one scene), expand to the broadest context, then narrow again to close.

Best for: features about unfamiliar topics, trend stories, profiles, explanatory journalism where the reader needs to be drawn in before the complexity arrives.

---

## Structure

### 1. Opening Anecdote (Narrow) — 1-3 paragraphs

Start small. One person, one scene, one moment that illustrates the story's theme. The reader doesn't yet know what the story is about — they just know they're interested in this person or situation.

Build trust by starting personal and concrete. Abstract introductions push readers away; specific ones pull them in.

### 2. Nut Graf (Widening) — 1-2 paragraphs

Explain why the story matters. Bridge from the specific opening to the universal. This is where the reader discovers that the anecdote they just read is a window into something much larger.

### 3. Body (Widest Point) — varies

Broaden to the full scope: detailed reporting, data, expert quotes, multiple sources, contextual information. This is the widest part of the diamond — maximum breadth.

Unlike the WSJ Formula's topically organized body, the Diamond allows for more exploratory organization. You're building the reader's understanding progressively. Each paragraph can introduce a new facet of the issue, each one broader than the last.

### 4. Return / Closing Anecdote (Narrowing) — 1-2 paragraphs

Converge back to the opening character or scene. The reader returns to familiar ground, but with new understanding. The same person or situation now carries the weight of everything the reader has learned.

---

## How It Differs from the WSJ Formula

They're siblings, not twins. The WSJ Formula is more structured (five distinct sections, a "to be sure" section, a clear evidence-building body). The Diamond is more fluid — it's about a shape (narrow → wide → narrow) rather than a checklist. The Diamond works better when the body needs to explore rather than argue.

Use the WSJ Formula when you have a thesis to prove. Use the Diamond when you have a landscape to explore.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Opening too broadly.** The narrow opening is the whole point. "Climate change affects millions" is not narrow. "The oak tree in Margaret Wu's backyard dropped its leaves three weeks early this year" is.
- **Forgetting to narrow at the end.** A Diamond without a return is just a feature with a nice opening. The closing needs to echo the opening to complete the shape.
- **Body that loses the thread.** The widest section can sprawl. Keep asking: does this paragraph serve the story established in the nut graf?
