# The Martini Glass

A hybrid of the Inverted Pyramid and narrative storytelling. Named for its shape: a wide top (summary delivering the news), a narrow stem (the transition), and a base that opens back up (chronological narrative). Related to the Hourglass but with a more gradual descent from summary into chronology, without the explicit mode-shift signal.

Best for: sequential news events (crime, disaster, court proceedings) where readers need the headline immediately but the chronological story is compelling enough to sustain a full telling.

---

## Structure

### 1. Summary Lede — 1-2 paragraphs

Deliver the most important information first, just like the Inverted Pyramid. Answer the essential Who, What, Where, When. The reader who stops here gets the news.

### 2. Nut Graf — 1-2 paragraphs

Context and significance. Why this matters, what the stakes are, what the reader needs to understand before the chronological telling begins.

### 3. Chronological Narrative — varies

Transition into telling the events in time order. Unlike the Hourglass, you don't need a hard "turn" with attribution — the shift can be gradual. Start from the most recent events and work backward, or start from the beginning and build forward.

Use scene-building techniques: specific times, locations, dialogue, sensory detail. The narrative section is where the story comes alive. The summary gave them the facts; this gives them the experience.

### 4. Strong Kicker — 1 paragraph

End with a memorable quote, a revealing detail, or a forward-looking statement. The Martini Glass earns a real ending — don't let the narrative trail off into minor details the way a pure Inverted Pyramid would.

---

## How It Differs from the Hourglass

The Hourglass has three distinct zones with a clear "turn" (a transitional phrase with attribution that explicitly signals the shift from summary to narrative). The Martini Glass blends more smoothly — the transition from summary to chronology is gradual rather than signaled.

In practice: if your story benefits from a clear "now let me tell you how it happened" moment, use the Hourglass. If the shift from summary to narrative can happen organically, the Martini Glass is more fluid.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Narrative section too thin.** If you're only going to spend two paragraphs on chronology, you don't need the Martini Glass — just write an Inverted Pyramid. The structure only earns its weight when the narrative section is substantial.
- **No kicker.** The whole point of adding narrative to the bottom is to give the piece a real ending. Don't squander that by trailing off.
- **Redundancy between summary and narrative.** The summary and narrative cover the same events from different angles. Be careful not to simply repeat the same information in a different order.
