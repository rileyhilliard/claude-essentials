# The Hourglass

Named by Roy Peter Clark at the Poynter Institute in 1983. A hybrid that serves both time-pressed scanners (with a news summary up top) and committed readers (with narrative depth below). The best of both worlds when your story has both urgency and a rich backstory.

Best for: crime reports, court cases, disaster stories, any story that needs both immediate news delivery and chronological depth.

---

## Structure

### 1. The Top (Summary) — 4-6 paragraphs

Deliver the news in a summary lede, answering the reader's most pressing questions. This section functions like a mini inverted pyramid — enough information that a time-pressed reader can leave satisfied.

Cover: what happened, who was involved, what the immediate consequences are. Don't hold back the headline.

### 2. The Turn — 1 sentence or short paragraph

A transitional phrase containing attribution that signals a shift from summary to narrative. This is the waist of the hourglass — the pinch point where the mode changes.

**Examples of turns:**
- "According to police, the events unfolded this way:"
- "Eyewitnesses described what happened next."
- "Court documents filed Thursday reconstruct the sequence of events."

The turn needs attribution because you're about to tell a story. The reader needs to know whose account they're getting.

### 3. The Narrative — varies

A chronological retelling complete with detail, dialogue, and background. Unlike the inverted pyramid's taper, this section builds. It rewards the reader who stayed.

This is where you deploy scene-building, sensory detail, and reconstructed dialogue. The summary section gave them the facts; this section gives them the experience.

End with a real ending — a strong quote, a revealing detail, a circle back to the opening. Don't just trail off. The hourglass earns a kicker.

---

## Why It Works

The inverted pyramid serves the scanner. Narrative serves the engaged reader. The hourglass serves both without compromising either. The scanner gets what they need in the top section and leaves. The engaged reader is rewarded with a richer story below.

The key insight: you don't have to choose between informing efficiently and telling a compelling story. You can do both, sequentially.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Making the top too long.** If your summary section runs past 6 paragraphs, you're delaying the narrative too long. Trim.
- **Weak turn.** The transition from summary to narrative needs to be clear. If the reader doesn't notice the mode shifting, they'll be confused by the sudden chronological telling.
- **Flat narrative.** The bottom half is where your best writing should live. If it reads like a police report, you've squandered the format. Use scene, dialogue, detail.
