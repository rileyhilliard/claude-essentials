# The 9 Structure

Coined by former Los Angeles Times editor Richard E. Meyer. Named for its shape: the story starts partway through the timeline at the most gripping moment, loops back to the beginning, then continues forward past the opening scene to resolution. The numeral "9" traces the path.

Best for: narrative journalism with a strong dramatic moment. Stories where there's a clear climax that can hook readers if previewed before full context is provided.

---

## Structure

### 1. Find the Cliffhanger

Lay out the full story on a timeline. Identify the most gripping moment — ideally no more than a third of the way down the timeline. This is where the reader will enter the story.

### 2. Open with the Cliffhanger — 2-4 paragraphs

Write the dramatic moment first. Stop at or just before the "all-is-lost" moment, before the outcome is revealed. You're stretching the rubber band of tension — the reader wants to know both how the character got here AND what happens next.

### 3. Rewind — 1 transition paragraph

Signal the flashback clearly: "It had started three months earlier," or "To understand what brought her to that ledge, go back to February." The reader needs to know you're rewinding intentionally.

### 4. Build Forward Chronologically — varies

Write from the true beginning up to the point where the opening scene occurred. Fill in the backstory, character development, and context that the reader now craves because they already saw the climax.

### 5. Continue Past the Opening — varies

Push through the moment you opened with and continue to resolution. The reader has been waiting for this since paragraph one.

---

## Why It Works

The 9 Structure front-loads the most compelling material. Instead of asking the reader to trust that the story will eventually get interesting, you prove it immediately and then earn the right to slow down and build context. The loop creates a narrative contract: "I showed you something gripping. Now let me explain how we got there."

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Opening moment isn't dramatic enough.** If the cliffhanger doesn't create genuine suspense, the rewind feels like a detour. The opening must be viscerally compelling.
- **Too much time before the rewind.** Get to the rewind quickly. The cliffhanger is 2-4 paragraphs, not 10.
- **Unclear flashback signal.** The reader must understand that you're rewinding on purpose. Ambiguity here creates confusion, not suspense.
- **Chronological slog after the rewind.** The backstory section still needs pacing and scene-building. Don't let it become a summary just because the reader is waiting for the payoff.
